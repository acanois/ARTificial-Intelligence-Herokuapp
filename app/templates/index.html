Mark Rothko

Mark Rothko was born in Latvia in 1903. At ten years old, he immigrated to the U.S. with his parents. Growing up in Portland, Oregon, Rothko graduated high school early with a mastery of four languages. Interested in politics, Rothko was heavily involved in labor and women’s rights movements. He also joined a theatre troupe. As an artist, Rothko redefined abstract expressionism, and was the grandfather of the new Washington Color School, of which Morris Louis was the preeminent figure. His signature style of stacked, fuzzy rectangles came about as a gradual evolution starting with figurative watercolors. Rothko is without doubt among the most important painters in the history of the world.

Morris Louis

Morris Louis was a painter working in Washington D.C. during the 50’s and 60’s. His paintings are the quintessential example of the Washington Color School, a group of artists in the D.C. area that rejected the painterly, impasto works of the abstract expressionists in favor of a flat, light style. Louis pioneered the use of a formaldehyde based acrylic paint known as Magna that he poured on his canvases. Unwittingly filling his small studio with toxic fumes from the paint, Louis died of lung cancer in 1962.
